The messy problem

Global fresh supply chains move product through a long line of hands. A container of fruit lands in one country, clears inbound QC, sits in a warehouse, moves to ripening, then gets packed into thousands of individual boxes that ship to customers. Along the way the unit of measure keeps changing, container to pallet to box, and the quality evidence lives in a separate world from the material itself.

So nobody can answer the question that actually matters: this box in my hand, where did it come from, who inspected it, and was it good?

Why it mattered

When you cannot connect a box back to its source, you cannot prove quality, manage a recall, settle a dispute, or trust your own inventory numbers. Everything downstream is guesswork.

Users and stakeholders

  • Field inspectors at origin
  • Inbound QC and warehouse teams
  • Ripening and packing teams
  • Management watching live stock and traceability
  • The end customer who wants to trust the product

Workflow I designed

  1. Product inspected and logged at the origin farm gate with a quality score
  2. Pushed across the border to inbound QC, checked against the origin result
  3. Broken down in the warehouse as the unit converts from container to pallets
  4. Some pallets move to ripening, some hold, tracked as split inventory
  5. Ripened pallets packed into individual boxes, the unit converting again
  6. Any box queried later returns its full chain history back to the source farm

Product decisions I made

  • Model any supply chain as a configurable chain of nodes, so the same product fits a fruit import or a grain network without a rebuild
  • Make material move node to node with a confirmed push, and auto convert the unit at every boundary, with a confirmation prompt before it commits
  • Support split inventory, because one container is rarely in one state. Some pallets ripen while others wait and others ship
  • Treat reverse traceability as the north star: given a box, return its pallet, its container, its origin inspection, and its quality score
  • Flag a reconciliation error the instant quantities across nodes stop adding up to the original

Intelligence layer

A chain and node data model with automatic unit of measure conversion, a live inventory balance that updates on every push, split state support, reconciliation checks, and a reverse traceability query that resolves a single box to its origin and its quality score. Computer vision and inspection data attach to the right node so quality travels with the material.

Product surfaces

  • Field and node mobile app
  • Live inventory dashboard per node and per lot
  • Unit conversion confirmation prompts
  • Reverse traceability query
  • Management traceability dashboard

What shipped

  • A configurable chain and node engine running in production
  • Live inventory balance across every node, with reconciliation alerts
  • Box level reverse traceability from packing floor back to the origin farm
  • Inspection evidence tied to the exact unit it describes
  • Proven on a real cross border fresh produce chain, extensible to any commodity

Business impact

Management can see exactly how much stock sits at each node in real time, and trace any single box back to the farm that grew it and the score it earned. This is the foundation for a world where a brand's own customers can trace the fruit in their hand back to source.

What this proves about me

I can design the hard data model under a real supply chain, unit conversions, split inventory, reconciliation, reverse traceability, and ship it to production, not just diagram it.

The messy problem

Manual food quality inspection is slow, subjective, and small sample. An inspector eyeballs a handful of berries or nuts, makes a call, and the rest of the batch is assumed to match. Two inspectors disagree, recalls slip through, and there is no audit trail.

At industrial volume, that variance is margin lost and brand risk taken on every shipment.

Why it mattered

For a premium produce brand, a wrong quality call is a recall, a rejected load, or a damaged reputation. Consistency at scale is the whole game.

Users and stakeholders

  • Quality teams at large produce brands
  • Plant and line operators running inspections
  • Buyers and suppliers settling on grade
  • QA leaders who need an audit trail

Workflow I designed

  1. Operator loads a sample into the analyser
  2. The device captures and the AI platform assesses in real time
  3. Defects detected and graded at the per defect level against the brand's spec
  4. Result and evidence stored to a verifiable quality database
  5. Models retrain on live samples through the feedback loop

Product decisions I made

  • Pair a purpose built hardware analyser with a cloud AI platform, so inspection is turnkey rather than a science project on the line
  • Grade at the per defect level per commodity, because a brand's spec is specific, not generic
  • Build an auto feedback loop into the models so they keep improving from live samples
  • Anchor everything to a verifiable quality database, so every assessment has an audit trail

Intelligence layer

A patented analyser feeding a proprietary auto ML platform. Deep learning models, trained on millions of images per commodity, do per defect grading in real time on a scalable architecture, with a built in feedback loop for continuous improvement.

Product surfaces

  • Turnkey hardware analyser
  • Cloud quality dashboard
  • Per defect grading reports
  • Verifiable quality database with audit trail

What shipped

  • Autonomous grading deployed across 30 plus analysers and 10 plus commodities
  • In use by leading global berry and nut brands
  • Models trained on millions of images and data points per commodity

Business impact

Roughly 4x lower operational cost than manual inspection and around 3x larger sample size, which means better quality calls and fewer recalls. Inspection went from a person's judgement to a consistent, auditable machine decision.

What this proves about me

I can drive a hardware plus AI product for demanding global brands, where the bar is industrial reliability and the output has to be trusted on the line every single time.

The messy problem

Manual commodity grading is subjective, slow, expert dependent, and impossible to audit later. The same sample gets two grades from two graders, and procurement pays against that variance.

Scaling it across locations, seasons, and crops only multiplies the inconsistency.

Why it mattered

When a grade sets a price, variance is money and disputes. The output has to be consistent, fast, and defensible.

Users and stakeholders

  • Procurement teams paying against grade
  • Quality labs and human graders
  • Field operators running capture
  • Buyers who need a defensible grade per lot

Workflow I designed

  1. Sample prepared under a controlled SOP
  2. Image captured on a calibrated device, with a quality gate before inference
  3. Model returns grade, defects, score, and confidence
  4. Result stored against batch metadata, fully replayable
  5. Dashboards roll grades into procurement and quality trends

Product decisions I made

  • Treat trust as a launch requirement, not a later fix, because the output influences payment
  • Design capture SOPs and calibration with field teams, since a great model on a bad image is a bad product
  • Track false accept and false reject separately, because they carry different commercial cost
  • Define release criteria per commodity and geography, not one global bar

Intelligence layer

Computer vision models trained on expert labelled data for grade, defect detection, and scoring. Wrapped in an image quality gate, calibration, repeatability testing, and production monitoring for drift and expert agreement.

Product surfaces

  • Model monitoring views

What shipped

The messy problem

Large catering operations waste food at scale, with little measurement. Logging is manual when it happens. Nobody knows if the driver is overproduction, prep, or plate waste, or which dish, day, or kitchen is the problem.

Cold chain visibility was thin too. Temperature logs lived on paper, and supplier certificates lived in inboxes.

Why it mattered

You cannot reduce what you cannot see. And a kitchen running blind on temperature is a food safety risk, not just a cost.

Users and stakeholders

  • Kitchen staff mid service
  • Kitchen managers setting portions and prep
  • Operations leaders comparing sites
  • Vendor compliance teams

Workflow I designed

  1. Staff place waste on a connected scale and weight captures automatically
  2. Camera captures the waste image
  3. Model classifies waste type and dish with a stated reason
  4. Staff confirm or correct in one tap
  5. Bluetooth sensors stream temperature and humidity in the background
  6. Dashboards break waste down by day part, dish, location, vendor, and reason

Product decisions I made

  • Make capture take seconds, because staff are mid service and will skip anything slower
  • Use a multimodal API for waste classification at MVP rather than training a model with no data
  • Treat every user correction as a label that funds a future custom model
  • Bring audits, sensors, SOPs, and waste under one roof instead of five tools

Intelligence layer

A multimodal model classifies waste type and dish from images. Bluetooth and IoT sensors handle temperature and humidity with completeness checks. Analytics turn it into day part, dish, and location level insight.

Product surfaces

  • Kitchen capture app
  • Connected weighing scale
  • Bluetooth temperature and humidity sensors
  • Operations and sustainability dashboards

What shipped

  • Digitized the full catering chain: supplier audits, kitchen audits, cold chain, SOPs, and waste
  • Real time compliance and sustainability reporting
  • Dish and day part level waste analytics

Business impact

Deployments delivered 20 percent plus food waste reduction. Once a manager could see that a specific dish on a specific day was the problem, the fix was a portioning decision, not a campaign.

What this proves about me

I can stitch hardware, sensors, AI, and a field workflow into one product, and sequence the AI bet so it pays for the next version of itself.

The messy problem

Honey supply is fragmented and opaque. A brand buying at the plant has no real visibility into origin, flora, harvest time, or how a batch moved from a beekeeper through aggregators and traders to a vendor.

Without that, there is no real traceability story to stand behind, and no real grip on procurement cost or pricing.

Why it mattered

For a consumer brand, origin is trust. For procurement, visibility is price. The chain had neither.

Users and stakeholders

  • Beekeepers, often first time app users
  • Aggregators and traders
  • Vendors building large batches
  • Plant and management teams

Workflow I designed

  1. Beekeeper records harvest and sale with bee boxes, flora, dates, photo, and geo tagged farm location
  2. Trader or aggregator scans QR, records sales on behalf of beekeepers, and delivers lots to a vendor
  3. Vendor creates batches, fills, captures moisture, PO, and invoice, and dispatches
  4. Every transaction and location is captured and rolled into a management dashboard

Product decisions I made

  • Build a different app flow for each actor instead of forcing one generic app on everyone
  • Let any actor create a lot on behalf of a beekeeper, so onboarding never blocks the chain
  • Geo tag and timestamp every lot and every handoff
  • Capture future quality fields, like moisture and flora to glucose ratio, so the same rails carry quality later

Intelligence layer

QR based lot tracking, geo tagged lot creation, and full transaction capture, anchored to a public blockchain for tamper evident records. Reports export to Excel and CSV, with concise batch summaries and an actor engagement dashboard.

Product surfaces

  • Beekeeper app
  • Trader and aggregator app
  • Vendor app with batch creation and filling
  • QR scanning
  • Management dashboard with geo tagged map and flora wise procurement

What shipped

  • Geo tagged supply chain visibility from hive to plant
  • Management reporting with batch and actor level insight

Business impact

Around 1.2M dollars in annual value generated, with a reported lift in brand trust, a small drop in procurement cost from field data, and a small revenue gain from better, traceable pricing. For the detailed deck and walkthrough, reach out to me.
